\begin{abstract}
LLM-driven code search has produced impressive results on GPU ML kernels (GEMM,
attention, convolution), but the optimization patterns in scientific computing
are systematically different: bandwidth-bound stencils with halo tiling,
register-blocked all-pairs interactions, multi-field Boltzmann updates,
neighbour-list molecular dynamics with atomics, parallel-chain MCMC, and
multi-kernel nonlinear PDE solvers. We present \textsc{Metal-Sci}, a 9-task
benchmark of scientific Apple-Silicon Metal compute kernels spanning five
optimization regimes. Every task ships a CPU reference, a roofline ceiling,
and a naive but correct seed; the fitness signal is the geometric mean of
\emph{achieved/ceiling} across three problem sizes, with any tolerance
failure forcing the score to zero. We release the harness and seeds, and
report preliminary 5--25 iteration evolution runs for Claude Opus~4.7,
Gemini~3-Flash, and Gemini~3.1-Pro on an Apple M1 Pro. Frontier models
recover canonical optimisations on well-trodden tasks (threadgroup tiling
on N-body, BGK symmetry algebra on Lattice Boltzmann, template specialisation
on HMC) yet still mishandle Metal-specific concurrency syntax and stall at
single-digit percent of peak on irregular-memory tasks (Lennard-Jones with
cell lists). The benchmark is designed for fast iteration: each candidate
compiles, runs, verifies, and scores in seconds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

LLM code-search systems --- FunSearch~[1], AlphaEvolve~[2], and recent
kernel-generation work~[3] --- evaluate language models as
\emph{optimisers} of executable artefacts. The choice of artefact matters:
KernelBench targets PyTorch ML kernels (GEMM, attention, convolution,
elementwise) on CUDA, where canonical implementations are heavily represented
in pretraining data and the optimisation surface is dominated by tiling and
warp-level reductions.

Scientific computing exposes a different surface. Stencils are
bandwidth-bound and reward halo and temporal blocking. All-pairs
gravitational interactions are compute-bound and reward register blocking
plus shared-memory cooperative loads. Lattice Boltzmann methods
ping-pong nine distribution functions per cell with non-trivial pull-stream
indexing. Molecular dynamics with cell lists touches irregular memory through
atomic counters. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo runs many chains in parallel where
register pressure scales with the problem dimension $d$. Grad-Shafranov
solvers chain a max-reduction with a variable-coefficient stencil over many
Picard outer steps. Each pattern stresses a distinct dimension of the
compiler/scheduler/memory hierarchy, and the canonical optimisations cited in
the literature~[5,6,7,8] differ regime-to-regime.

We choose Apple Silicon's Metal compute pipeline as the target for three
reasons. First, it is underrepresented in CUDA-centric training data; we observe
that frontier models reliably mishandle Metal-specific syntax such as the
\texttt{[[max\_total\_threads\_per\_threadgroup]]} kernel attribute --- a
real out-of-distribution generalisation test. Second, the unified memory
model removes the host-device copy plumbing, enabling sub-second compile-run-verify
cycles per candidate that are essential for fast evolutionary loops.
Third, the architecture supports rich simdgroup intrinsics
(\texttt{simd\_max}, \texttt{simd\_broadcast}) and threadgroup-memory tiling,
giving the LLM a non-trivial optimisation surface.

\paragraph{Contributions.} (i) A 9-task scientific compute benchmark
spanning five optimisation regimes, each with a CPU reference,
roofline-anchored fitness function, and multi-size generalisation gate.
(ii) A runtime-compiled harness (no offline \texttt{xcrun metal} toolchain)
that exposes compile errors, correctness diagnostics, and per-size GPU
timing back to the LLM. (iii) Preliminary evolution runs across Claude
Opus~4.7 and Gemini~3 on M1 Pro, qualitatively analysing which patterns
each model recovers and which it misses.

\section{Benchmark tasks}
\label{sec:tasks}

Each task ships (a) a Metal source describing the problem, (b) a naive
correct seed, (c) a CPU reference with task-specific tolerance, (d) three
in-distribution sizes plus one held-out size, and (e) a roofline ceiling
in either GFLOPS (compute-bound) or GB/s (bandwidth-bound). Tasks are
grouped by optimisation regime.

\subsection{Regular stencils (regime R1)}

\paragraph{heat2d.} Two-dimensional heat equation, 5-point stencil on
$(N_x{\times}N_y)$ grid with Dirichlet boundaries. Per timestep, per
interior cell:
\begin{equation}
u^{n+1}_{i,j} \;=\; u^n_{i,j} + \alpha\!\left(u^n_{i-1,j}+u^n_{i+1,j}
   + u^n_{i,j-1}+u^n_{i,j+1} - 4\,u^n_{i,j}\right).
\end{equation}
Bandwidth-bound at the cache-perfect 8\,B/cell limit; tests halo handling
and temporal blocking. Sizes: $256^2$, $512^2$, $1024^2$.

\paragraph{wave3d.} Three-dimensional acoustic wave equation, 7-point
isotropic Laplacian, leapfrog in time:
\begin{equation}
u^{n+1}_{i,j,k} = 2u^n_{i,j,k} - u^{n-1}_{i,j,k}
   + \alpha\,\big(u^n_{i\pm 1,j,k}+u^n_{i,j\pm 1,k}+u^n_{i,j,k\pm 1}-6\,u^n_{i,j,k}\big),
\end{equation}
where each $\pm$ expands to two terms. CFL stability requires
$\alpha=(c\Delta t/\Delta x)^2<1/3$ (we use $0.18$).
Triple-buffered ping-pong; 12\,B/cell unique DRAM traffic. Tests register
pressure, 2.5D blocking, and choice of marching axis. Sizes: $64^3$,
$160^3$, $192^3$.

\subsection{Compute-bound (regime R2)}

\paragraph{nbody.} All-pairs gravitational $N$-body with leapfrog. Per
body $i$ per step:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{a}_i \;=\; G\sum_{j} m_j
   \frac{\mathbf{r}_j-\mathbf{r}_i}
        {(\|\mathbf{r}_j-\mathbf{r}_i\|^2+\varepsilon^2)^{3/2}},
\quad \mathbf{v}\!\mathrel{+}\!=\mathbf{a}\Delta t,
\quad \mathbf{r}\!\mathrel{+}\!=\mathbf{v}\Delta t.
\end{equation}
Self-interaction is masked by the softening $\varepsilon$. $20$\,FLOPs per
pair; ceiling at peak FP32 GFLOPS. Tests register blocking and threadgroup
cooperative loads. Sizes: $N\in\{256,1024,2048\}$, 10 steps.

\paragraph{hmc.} Hamiltonian Monte Carlo on an anisotropic Gaussian target,
$U(q)=\tfrac12 q^\top A q$ with $A=\Sigma^{-1}$. One thread per chain;
many chains in parallel. Each HMC step draws $p\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ and
runs $L$ leapfrog inner steps with stepsize $\varepsilon$,
\begin{equation}
p \leftarrow p - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}A q,\quad
q \leftarrow q + \varepsilon p,\quad
p \leftarrow p - \varepsilon Aq,\ \cdots,\quad
p \leftarrow p - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}A q,
\end{equation}
followed by a Metropolis accept/reject with $\log u_{\mathrm{acc}}<-\Delta H$.
Correctness is verified statistically (sample mean and Frobenius covariance
error vs.\ the target). Three sizes $(d, K)\in\{(8,16384),(16,4096),(32,1024)\}$
probe the register-pressure boundary; at $d{=}32$ the per-thread state
($\sim$512\,B) competes with the register file.

\subsection{Multi-field, exotic memory (regime R3)}

\paragraph{lbm.} D2Q9 Lattice Boltzmann, fused pull-stream + BGK
collision, periodic BC. With velocity table $\mathbf{c}_k$ and weights
$w_k$, per cell per step:
\begin{align}
f^{\mathrm{str}}_k(\mathbf{x})
   &= f^{\mathrm{in}}_k(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{c}_k),\quad
\rho = \textstyle\sum_k f^{\mathrm{str}}_k,\quad
\mathbf{u} = \tfrac{1}{\rho}\sum_k \mathbf{c}_k\,f^{\mathrm{str}}_k, \\
f^{\mathrm{eq}}_k &= w_k\rho\!\left(1+3(\mathbf{c}_k{\cdot}\mathbf{u})
   +\tfrac{9}{2}(\mathbf{c}_k{\cdot}\mathbf{u})^2 - \tfrac{3}{2}\|\mathbf{u}\|^2\right),\\
f^{\mathrm{out}}_k &= f^{\mathrm{str}}_k - \tau^{-1}\!\left(f^{\mathrm{str}}_k - f^{\mathrm{eq}}_k\right).
\end{align}
Storage is SoA: $f[k\,N_xN_y + jN_x + i]$. $72$\,B/cell DRAM traffic.
Tests AoS/SoA layout, push vs pull streaming, and algebraic
factorisations of the BGK kernel.

\paragraph{ising.} Two-dimensional Ising model, checkerboard Metropolis
Monte Carlo on a periodic $N_x\times N_y$ lattice with $\sigma\in\{\pm 1\}$
stored as int8. Per attempt at site $(i,j)$:
\begin{equation}
h = \sigma_{i\pm 1,j}+\sigma_{i,j\pm 1}\in\{-4,\ldots,4\},\quad
\Delta E = 2J\sigma\,h,\quad
\mathrm{accept}\iff u<\exp(-\beta\,\Delta E).
\end{equation}
A precomputed five-entry $p_{\mathrm{accept}}$ table and a counter-based
Murmur-fmix32 PRNG yield bit-exact CPU/GPU agreement: verification is
byte-equality on the spin array. $2$\,B/site/sweep ceiling.

\subsection{Irregular memory and atomics (regime R4)}

\paragraph{lj.} Lennard-Jones molecular dynamics with a cell-list spatial
hash. Three kernels per step --- \texttt{clear\_cells}, \texttt{build\_cells}
(atomic scatter), \texttt{step} (27-neighbour-cell pair force + integration):
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_i = \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)} -24\!\left(2r_{ij}^{-12}-r_{ij}^{-6}\right)r_{ij}^{-2}\,(\mathbf{r}_j-\mathbf{r}_i),
\qquad r_{ij}<r_{\mathrm{cut}}=2.5,
\end{equation}
with minimum-image periodic wrap. Cell occupancy is built via
\texttt{atomic\_fetch\_add} on a per-cell counter. Sizes: $K^3$
particles for $K\in\{12,16,22\}$; $N\in\{1728,4096,10648\}$.
Tests load balancing, atomic contention, and the irregular access patterns
characteristic of neighbour-list MD.

\subsection{Multi-kernel reductions (regime R5)}

\paragraph{gradshaf.} Grad-Shafranov fixed-boundary equilibrium via Picard
iteration, two kernels per outer step: an interior max-reduction
$\psi_{\mathrm{axis}}=\max_{(i,j)\in\mathrm{int}}\psi$, then a
variable-coefficient 5-point stencil with nonlinear source:
\begin{align}
\psi_{\mathrm{norm}} &= \psi/\psi_{\mathrm{axis}},\quad
J = R\,p_{\mathrm{axis}}\cdot 4\psi_{\mathrm{norm}}(1-\psi_{\mathrm{norm}})\cdot
   \mathbf{1}[0<\psi_{\mathrm{norm}}<1],\\
\Delta^*\psi &= a_W\psi_W+a_E\psi_E+a_N\psi_N+a_S\psi_S+a_C\psi_C,\\
\psi^{n+1} &= \psi^n + \omega\,(-\mu_0 R J - \Delta^*\psi)/a_C,
\end{align}
with $a_{W,E}=1/dR^2 \pm 1/(2RdR)$ ($R$-dependent: breaks east/west
symmetry), $a_{N,S}=1/dZ^2$, $a_C=-2(1/dR^2+1/dZ^2)$. Tests
in-kernel reduction strategies (single-TG vs simdgroup-tree) and multi-kernel
composition with implicit dispatch ordering. Sizes: $65^2$, $257^2$, $513^2$.

A bandwidth-bound smoke-test (\texttt{saxpy}, $y\!\leftarrow\!\alpha x+y$) is also
included to validate the harness. We omit it from the analysis.

\section{Harness}
\label{sec:harness}

\paragraph{Compile and dispatch.} The harness uses PyObjC's Metal bindings
and runtime-compiles \texttt{.metal} source via
\texttt{MTLDevice.newLibraryWithSource}, avoiding the offline
\texttt{xcrun metal}/\texttt{metallib} toolchain. Compilation errors are
returned as structured strings to the LLM. Buffers are allocated in unified
memory (\texttt{MTLResourceStorageModeShared}); host-side numpy views
alias buffer contents with a single copy on read-back. All dispatches for
a multi-size run share one \texttt{MTLCommandBuffer}, and timings are taken
from \texttt{GPUEndTime}\,$-$\,\texttt{GPUStartTime} (not wall clock).
Each measurement uses 3 warmup and 10 timed dispatches; we report the
median across timed runs.

\paragraph{Hardware introspection.} The chip is detected via \texttt{sysctl}
\texttt{machdep.cpu.brand\_string} and looked up in a per-family table of
peak FP32 GFLOPS and DRAM bandwidth (M1 through M4, base/Pro/Max/Ultra).
The lookup gives the roofline for each task at registration time. All runs
in this paper are on Apple M1 Pro (4500\,GFLOPS, 200\,GB/s).

\paragraph{Scoring.} For a candidate that passes correctness at every size,
the score is the geometric mean of $\mathrm{achieved}/\mathrm{ceiling}$
across in-distribution sizes:
$
S = \left(\prod_{i} f_i\right)^{1/n}
$
with $f_i\in[0,1]$. Any tolerance failure forces $S=0$; this hard gate
prevents the search from trading correctness for speed. The geometric
mean across sizes (rather than a single problem size) discourages
overfitting to one regime.

\paragraph{Evolution loop.} A single-population $(\mu{=}1+\lambda{=}1)$
loop. Iteration $i$ shows the LLM the previous candidate, the incumbent
best, a short history of correctness/score per iteration, and the original
task spec. The LLM returns a single Metal source block (multi-kernel tasks
emit multiple \texttt{kernel} functions in one file). The harness compiles,
verifies at all sizes, times, and promotes the candidate if it strictly
beats the incumbent. We persist prompts, raw responses, extended-thinking
tokens, extracted source, and per-size JSON results to disk. The
\texttt{call\_llm} bridge dispatches Claude via the Agent SDK and Gemini
via \texttt{google-genai} from a single entry point.

The LBM 256$^2$ result above 100\% of the published ceiling reflects working-set
residency in Apple Silicon's system-level cache: the 8\,B/cell roofline is
DRAM-only, while the streamed 9-distribution working set fits in SLC at this
size. We report it unmodified to flag the limitation of the static roofline
in the multi-field, multi-step regime.

\paragraph{Qualitative findings.} Three patterns recur across runs.
\emph{(i) Models recover canonical optimisations on well-trodden tasks.}
On nbody, Gemini-3.1-Pro one-shots threadgroup-memory tiling with
cooperative load and 4-way ILP unroll (Nyland 2007); on LBM, both Claude
4.6 and 4.7 independently find the BGK pairwise-symmetry algebraic
factorisation; on HMC, Opus~4.7 introduces a \texttt{template <uint D>}
worker dispatched on $d$ at iteration 6, taking $d{=}8$ from
121 to 970\,GFLOPS in a single iteration by enabling full unroll of the
inner $A q$ matvec.
\emph{(ii) Metal-specific syntax is a real OOD failure.} On LBM, Opus~4.6
mishandles the \texttt{[[max\_total\_threads\_per\_threadgroup(N)]]}
attribute placement on six independent iterations (the same compile error
each time), missing a structural lever Opus~4.7 uses correctly on its first
attempt. On gradshaf, an early run hit 6/10 NaN failures because the model
hard-coded a $32{\times}8$ tile against a host-pinned $16{\times}16$
threadgroup; an explicit task-spec note about dispatch geometry steered the
next run cleanly past the trap.
\emph{(iii) Irregular tasks remain hard.} Lennard-Jones with cell lists sits
at $\sim$1\% of FP32 peak after 10 iterations across both Claude and Gemini.
The atomic scatter in \texttt{build\_cells} and the variable-occupancy
neighbour-cell loop are not patterns the models recombine effectively in our
budget; this is the largest gap in the suite and an obvious target for
search-strategy work.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why a roofline-anchored score?}
A single fraction-of-ceiling per size answers ``how close are we to the
hardware?'' in physical units, and the geometric mean across sizes gives
a single scalar that is hard to game by overfitting one regime. By
contrast, raw GFLOPS rewards bigger problems and obscures the
small-problem/launch-overhead regime that scientific workflows typically
care about.

\paragraph{Why Apple Silicon?}
The unified memory model halves the host-side scaffolding compared to
discrete-GPU CUDA, enabling sub-second compile-run-verify cycles per
candidate. This matters more than it sounds: an evolutionary loop with
50\,iterations and 10 candidates per generation needs a fast harness, not
a fast kernel. Metal also exposes a smaller and quirkier surface than CUDA
(simdgroup intrinsics, threadgroup attributes, no explicit shared-memory
sizing), which surfaces real OOD failures of CUDA-trained LLMs on tasks
that are otherwise textbook.

\paragraph{Limitations.} (i) The ceilings are static per-chip family lookups
and do not account for system-level cache residency at small sizes
(see the LBM 256$^2$ result above). A workload-aware roofline
[4] per task and per size would tighten the score.
(ii) The single-population $(1{+}1)$ loop is the simplest evolutionary
strategy and tends to plateau within 10--25 iterations on most tasks; an
island-model or FunSearch-style~[1] archive is the
natural next experiment.
(iii) We do not attempt to detect verbatim retrieval of canonical kernels
(Nyland N-body, etc.) and currently treat ``model recalled the answer'' and
``model optimised'' identically.

\paragraph{Future work.} Adding sparse linear algebra (SpMV, CG) covers the
last regime in the roadmap; cross-chip generalisation (evolve on M2~Pro,
evaluate on M3~Max) measures how chip-specific the discovered optimisations
are; and adding a novelty signal in the spirit of [2] should help
on the irregular-memory tasks where the search currently plateaus.
